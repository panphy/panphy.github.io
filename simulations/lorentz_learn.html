Why Lorentz transformations matter

At everyday speeds, Newtonian ideas work well. But when objects move close to the speed of light, measurements of space and time depend on the observer‚Äôs frame of reference.

The Lorentz transformation tells us exactly how to convert coordinates between two inertial frames, usually written as a rest frame \(S\) and a moving frame \(S'\) with relative speed \(v\).

In this app, velocities are shown as a fraction of \(c\), so \(\beta = v/c\).

Core equations

For motion along one spatial dimension:

$$x' = \gamma (x - vt), \qquad t' = \gamma\left(t - \frac{vx}{c^2}\right)$$ $$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}$$

These equations explain time dilation, length contraction, and relativity of simultaneity as one consistent geometry in spacetime.

How to read the Minkowski diagram

  • The vertical axis is \(ct\) and the horizontal axis is \(x\).
  • The light cone lines \(ct=\pm x\) are the same in every inertial frame.
  • The primed axes tilt when \(v\neq 0\): this visualizes how space and time mix.
  • Events are points in spacetime. Their \((x,t)\) and \((x',t')\) values are both shown.

Velocity transformation

If an object moves at speed \(u\) in \(S\), then its speed in \(S'\) is:

$$u' = \frac{u-v}{1-\frac{uv}{c^2}}$$
